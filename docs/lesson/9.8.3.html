Alerting Strategies and On-Call Best Practices

Intuition

Good alerting is like a smoke detector—it goes off only when there is real danger. Bad alerting is like a car alarm that triggers from wind, training everyone to ignore it. On-call is sustainable only when alerts are meaningful and the team has the tools to respond quickly.

Alerting Strategies & On-Call Best Practices

Effective alerting and on-call practices are critical for maintaining reliable systems. Poor alerting leads to alert fatigue, missed incidents, and burned-out engineers. Good alerting is actionable, prioritized, and tied to user impact.

Core Concepts

  • Symptom-Based Alerting: Alert on symptoms (high error rate, elevated latency) rather than causes (high CPU, disk usage). Symptoms directly correlate with user impact; causes may or may not affect users.
  • Multi-Window, Multi-Burn-Rate Alerts: Instead of simple threshold alerts, alert when error budget burn rate exceeds a threshold over multiple time windows (e.g., 2% budget consumed in 1 hour OR 5% in 6 hours). This reduces false positives while catching both fast and slow burns.
  • Alert Severity Levels: Page (wake someone up) only for user-facing issues that need immediate action. Use tickets for issues that can wait until business hours. Use dashboards for informational signals.
  • On-Call Rotation: Distribute on-call duty fairly across the team. Typical rotation: 1 week primary, 1 week secondary, N weeks off. Compensate on-call engineers with pay or time off.
  • Incident Response: Follow a structured process: detect, triage, mitigate, resolve, review. Write blameless postmortems that focus on systemic improvements, not individual fault.

Key Takeaways

  1. Every page should be urgent, actionable, and require a human—if an alert can be auto-remediated, automate it.
  2. Track alert-to-incident ratio. If fewer than 50% of pages result in action, you have an alert quality problem.
  3. Blameless postmortems and reducing toil are the best investments for sustainable on-call.

Exercises

  1. Implement Multi-Window Burn Rate Alerts

    Using Prometheus and Alertmanager, create burn-rate alerts for a 99.9% availability SLO. Define two alert rules: a fast-burn alert (14.4x burn rate over 1 hour, 5-minute short window) and a slow-burn alert (6x burn rate over 3 days, 1-hour short window). Test by injecting errors.

    Hint

    Fast burn: (1 - (sum(rate(http_requests_total{code!~'5..'}[1h])) / sum(rate(http_requests_total[1h])))) > (14.4 * 0.001). Use the short window as an AND condition to ensure the issue is ongoing, not a brief spike.

  2. Build a Runbook for a Critical Alert

    Write a detailed runbook for a 'High Error Rate' alert. Include: what the alert means, how to verify the issue, likely root causes with diagnostic commands, mitigation steps (rollback, scale up, circuit break), escalation paths, and communication templates.

    Hint

    Structure: (1) Alert description and SLO impact, (2) Verification steps (curl to health endpoint, check Grafana dashboard link), (3) Common causes with kubectl, pg_stat_activity, and log query commands, (4) Mitigation playbook, (5) Escalation contacts.

Common Problems & Solutions

1 On-call engineers are paged multiple times per night, leading to burnout and attrition.
Cause: Too many noisy alerts that are not actionable, auto-remediable issues paging humans, or insufficient alert deduplication.
Solution: Audit all alerts: delete or convert to tickets any alert that was not actionable in the past 30 days. Implement auto-remediation for known transient issues. Group related alerts to reduce page volume. Target fewer than 2 pages per on-call shift.
2 A major outage goes undetected for 30 minutes because no alert fires.
Cause: Alerting is based on cause-based metrics (CPU, memory) that did not spike, while user-facing symptoms (error rate, latency) were not monitored.
Solution: Implement symptom-based alerting tied to SLOs. Add synthetic monitoring (health checks from external locations) as a backup detection mechanism. Alert on error budget burn rate, which captures any reliability degradation regardless of root cause.
